# Wireframing

Having decided on our web app concept, I employed early wireframes to figure out the scope of the design and whether or not it was feasible as a clear and engaging application. At this stage, I focused on the core high-usage functions of the web app that were essential to the purpose of the design, and left surrounding details rough.

Brainstorming possible core functions of the web app, example user flows that came up were:
- Users log in
- Users set their injury and recovery context
- Recovery tracking starts with documented stages
- And users then use the app to find relevant information from people with similar experiences

While I first interpreted it as a personal tracker through discussion within my group, breaking down the necessary functions and user flow helped me think of it as a Recovery Community Hub where users can focus on their personal recovery while learning from others' experiences. This opened up possibilities for future development of the prototype.

With this in mind I created the first wireframes of the Home, Explore and Recovery pages.

## Core Requirements
The homepage was designed as a snapshot of the user's recovery stages and progress, helping them understand their context and next actions. To support this thumbnail of the user's recovery, the actual recovery page features a detailed timeline and suggestions on the user's recovery, such as exercises and habits. The Explore page focused on discovery, using the search bar and various filter options so users can find posts from people with similar experiences. The page also starts at a suggested tab with less specific posts about others' injuries.

Through these wireframes, I identified multiple functional requirements for our prototype. Users need to be able to set and update their recovery context, including injury details, recovery stage, and goals. The app needs to show recovery progress clearly from the homepage, while letting users document their recovery through journal-like logs. Finally, users should be able to find related discussions about their injuries and post about it themselves, reinforcing the social value of the web app.

At the same time, the wireframe also revealed possible weaknesses in some functions. Since this wireframe was developed a bit before the previous blog, an example of this is the generation of medical plans and automated recommendation algorithms. As the prototype is aimed towards the image of a community hub for people recovering from health complications, automated advice takes away from its aspects of discovery and leans towards a professional health advisor, which it isn't qualified to do. Therefore, this made it an optional feature rather than core.

With this iterative stage, I was able to clarify possible user flows and navigation for the prototype, which makes it easier to understand and use. I also made our concept more concrete and drew out specific needs while getting rid of the weeds of the early concepts. Optional features like recommendation logistics and rehab plans made it hard to focus on the front-facing requirements essential to our design and hindered its feasibility.
